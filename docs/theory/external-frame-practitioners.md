# External Frame Practitioners

This note identifies successful public writers and practitioners who use frames,
metaphors, stories, or narrative structures to make abstract situations easier
to reason about. It is a benchmark map for FRAMES, not an endorsement list.

## Claim Boundary

These practitioners are useful because they show how frames travel. Their public
success does not prove every method is cognitively or ethically reliable.
FRAMES should borrow design constraints, not rhetorical certainty.

## Practitioner Map

| Practitioner | Known For | Useful FRAMES Lesson | Main Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| George Lakoff | Conceptual metaphor and political framing. | Frames are not decoration; they shape what people notice and consider natural. | Can tempt users to treat framing as persuasion warfare. |
| Chip Heath and Dan Heath | Sticky ideas, change, decision, and moment design. | Make frames simple, concrete, emotional, credible, and story-shaped enough to be reused. | Stickiness can outrun evidence. |
| Nancy Duarte | Presentation story structure and audience-centered persuasion. | Treat the audience as the hero and the communicator as guide; contrast current state with possible future. | High persuasion power requires strong evidence boundaries. |
| Annette Simmons | Trust, influence, and six kinds of stories. | Different story jobs exist: identity, motive, vision, values, teaching, and anticipated objection. | Personal stories can become manipulative if motive is false. |
| Stephen Denning | Leadership storytelling and organizational change. | Small, concrete springboard stories can help people imagine a different future. | Leadership stories can overpromise change without operating detail. |
| Robert McKee | Story structure, conflict, protagonist, point of view. | A frame needs conflict, values, point of view, and stakes to become memorable. | Drama conventions can overheat ordinary work. |
| Donald Miller / StoryBrand | Customer-as-hero marketing structure. | Clarify protagonist, problem, guide, plan, call to action, and success/failure stakes. | Marketing clarity can flatten complex stakeholder systems. |
| Malcolm Gladwell | Narrative popularization of social-science ideas. | Pair surprising cases with a memorable model; use stories to make abstract patterns portable. | Anecdotes can feel like proof when they are only illustration. |
| Jonathan Haidt | Moral psychology and intuitive metaphors such as rider/elephant. | Some frames work because they make hidden mental roles visible. | Moral frames can polarize if used to label rather than understand. |
| Rory Sutherland | Behavioral framing and non-obvious value perception. | Small changes in frame can change perceived value without changing the object. | Can drift toward cleverness detached from accountability. |

## What FRAMES Should Borrow

### Frame Jobs Before Story Shape

Borrow from Annette Simmons and Duarte: stories have jobs. A frame should say
whether it is building trust, teaching a behavior, creating vision, surfacing an
objection, or making a decision feel navigable.

### Audience As Participant

Borrow from Duarte and StoryBrand: the audience should know their role in the
scene. This reinforces [perspective-metadata.md](perspective-metadata.md):
operator, peer, protected party, owner, learner, steward, responder, reviewer.

### Concrete Transfer

Borrow from the Heath brothers and Gladwell: concrete, vivid scenes travel
better than abstractions. But FRAMES must add an evidence boundary so vividness
does not become proof.

### Conflict and Stakes

Borrow from McKee: frames become memorable when a value is under pressure. But
FRAMES should keep ordinary work from being inflated into emergency, combat, or
heroic drama unless the stakes justify it.

### Moral and Authority Awareness

Borrow from Lakoff and Haidt: frames often carry moral and authority structures.
FRAMES should expose those structures explicitly instead of letting them ride
inside the analogy unnoticed.

### Objection-Aware Story

Borrow from Simmons: the strongest frame may name what the audience is already
thinking. This is especially useful for cross-team conflict, organizational
change, and leadership communication.

## Design Implications

| FRAMES Surface | Implication |
|---|---|
| `FrameEntry` | Future fields should support perspective, source family, risk band, and claim strength before persuasion-oriented metadata. |
| Fit rubric | Add checks for story job, audience role, and whether vividness exceeds evidence. |
| Application packs | Leadership and product packs should require alternate frames for contested decisions. |
| AI-agent use | AI should return a frame with action, evidence boundary, misuse warning, and at least one alternate for medium/high-risk contexts. |
| Research grounding | Public claims should separate practitioner success from scientific validation. |
